Let’s look at the cold, hard operational realities of why the corporate-wide dream is dead on arrival, and what a realistic micro-success looks like.

Why "Every Location" is a Corporate Impossibility
1. The "Supplier Lock-In" Monopoly
Planet Fitness is a publicly traded, multi-billion-dollar franchise machine. They don't buy equipment from different vendors on a whim. They have massive, exclusive, global procurement contracts with commercial fitness giants like Matrix Fitness and Life Fitness.

These contracts dictate exactly how many treadmills, ellipticals, and stationary bikes go into a standard layout.

Step Revolution (the creators of SMX) is an indie amusement company, not a corporate fitness conglomerate. They cannot fulfill or support a multi-thousand-location hardware deployment with corporate service-level agreements (SLAs).

2. The Maintenance Nightmare
A standard gym floor operates on predictable maintenance. If a treadmill belt rips, a certified local technician swaps it out under a standard warranty contract.

If a load cell or sensor on an SMX stage goes dead or needs recalibration because someone dropped a 45-pound plate on it, a standard fitness technician will have absolutely no idea what to do.

Gyms hate downtime. A broken machine taking up premium real estate is an eyesore that generates member complaints.

3. The Litigious "Purple Carpet" Culture
Planet Fitness protects its brand image aggressively.

The Noise: SMX requires thumping music or headphones. If a user is stomping out a 150 BPM song on a metal platform, it disrupts the quiet, casual environment PF tries to cultivate.

The Software Liability: SMX contains licensed music tracks. Corporate compliance departments sweat bullets over music licensing, screen safety warnings, and liability waivers for interactive software.

What IS Realistic? (The Rogue Franchise Loophole)
If you scale your ambition down from "changing the corporate mandate" to "convincing one specific owner who owns a handful of regional clubs," the odds shift from 0% to maybe 5% to 10%.

Planet Fitness operates heavily on regional franchise groups (companies that own and operate 10 to 50 locations in a specific territory). These franchise groups have a slight amount of operational leeway, provided they meet corporate's core layout requirements.

If an owner is desperate to differentiate their specific clubs from a local Crunch Fitness or YMCA, they might entertain an experimental "Exergaming" or "HIIT Interactive" corner.

You do the legwork with Step Revolution: You'd need to coordinate with Step Revolution to see if they even have a "Non-Coin-Op Fitness/Home" build that strips out arcade styling and uses a clean, commercial health-club UI.

You find an open-minded Regional Franchise Director: You locate a franchise owner who is personally a gamer or interested in tech-forward fitness trends.

You treat it as a black-box test: You pitch it for one specific club in a college town or a high-density area where the demographic matches.

The Verdict
Forget about changing Planet Fitness corporate. They are an oil tanker; you cannot turn them.

But if you want to treat this like an intense B2B sales hustle—building a pitch deck, targeting franchise owners on LinkedIn, and fighting through a wall of "no"—you might, just maybe, convince a single ambitious franchise group to run a trial run in one room.
